For this category we will be showing diagrams about competitive programming problems and solutions.

Number of accepted solutions per level

The first visualization is a Pie chart with the number of accepted solutions for each difficulty level in Codechef.

The result shows, as expected, that the hardest level's (challenge) solutions are the fewest in number due to the problems' difficulty and the most accepted solutions are in the easy difficulty. This means that users who are probably new to Competitive Programming do not go to beginner problems as much and instead opt for something harder, to keep both the enjoyability of writing code and simultaneously learning new ways to solve problems without burning their brains out.

Tested vs Untested solutions

CodeChef has an automated system for compiling, running, and testing solutions, but also invites experienced users to manually run test cases and create solutions, compensating them with a small fee. This visualization underscores the importance of testing. Manual test case handling minimizes errors caused by factors other than incorrect code, such as poorly written test cases.

For accepted solutions, the higher number of tested problems suggests that differences between tested and untested solutions may stem from poorly written automated test cases or incorrect problem statements. Additionally, programs with runtime errors are fewer among tested solutions, highlighting the benefits of manual testing.

Most difficult problems - Top 25

CodeChef problems are distinguished by difficulty and tags, with tags indicating the required algorithms. The diagram shows the percentage of accepted solutions for each tag.

Problems involving recursion and prime numbers are the most difficult, indicated by their low solution percentages. In contrast, problems related to sorting, binary search, and algebra are easier to solve.

Overall, the tags show high difficulty, as evidenced by the generally low percentages of accepted solutions.
